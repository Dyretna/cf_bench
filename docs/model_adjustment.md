# Adjusting the Model vs. Adjusting the Counterfactual Objective
*A practical guide for improving counterfactual recommendations*

This document explains why counterfactuals sometimes fail to reach the target risk, and how you can intervene either on the **model** or on the **counterfactual objective** to make CF generation more realistic and achievable.

---

## 1. Background

In a counterfactual recommendation system, a predictive model outputs a probability:
- P(y = 1 | x)


Counterfactuals are generated by modifying input features until the predicted risk falls below a target threshold. Our rule is:

- target_risk = 0.5 × original_risk


This works when the original risk is moderately high.
But when the original risk is already low (e.g., 0.14 → 0.07), the target becomes extremely difficult to reach. As a result:

- Many counterfactuals fail.
- True positive cases often have low predicted risk.
- The CF engine appears ineffective even when it makes reasonable changes.

This is not a failure of the CF algorithm — it is a mismatch between the **model’s probability distribution** and the **CF objective**.

---

## 2. Two Levers You Can Adjust

There are two fundamentally different ways to address this issue.

### 2.1 Adjusting the Model

This means changing how the model distributes probabilities.
Examples include:

- Training on balanced data
- Using class weights
- Changing the loss function
- Calibrating probabilities after training

The goal is to make the model more willing to assign higher probabilities to positive cases. This increases the original risk for true positives, which makes the CF target more reachable.

### 2.2 Adjusting the Counterfactual Objective

Instead of changing the model, you can change what counts as a “successful” counterfactual.
Examples include:

- Using a minimum floor for the target risk
- Using an absolute reduction instead of a relative one
- Using a fixed threshold (e.g., “reduce risk below 0.20”)
- Using hybrid rules

This prevents unrealistic targets such as requiring a drop from 0.14 to 0.07, which may be impossible even with meaningful lifestyle changes.

---

## 3. Why This Matters

Many positive cases receive low predicted probabilities because the model is conservative. When the CF objective demands a very large reduction relative to an already low value, the CF engine cannot succeed.

By adjusting either:

- **the model** (to produce higher probabilities for positives), or
- **the CF objective** (to avoid unrealistic targets),

you make counterfactual recommendations more stable, interpretable, and achievable.

---

## 4. Summary

- Low predicted risks for positive cases make CF targets too strict.
- You can fix this by modifying the **model** or the **CF target rule**.
- Adjusting the CF objective is often the simplest and most transparent solution.
